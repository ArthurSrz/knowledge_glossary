[Share transfer clauses](https://www.legifrance.gouv.fr/codes/article_lc/LEGIARTI000020148461)

Share transfer clauses are the building blocks that govern who may enter and leave a company's ownership, and they reveal the real legal line — not between *types* of pact, but between what belongs in a [[Shareholders agreement]] and what must sit in the by-laws to gain full force. **Approval clause** (*agrément*): a transfer may be subject to the company's prior approval; under art. L.228-23 Code de commerce **a transfer made in breach of a statutory approval clause is null**, a radical sanction the by-laws alone confer — in a mere pact, breach yields only damages. **Forced sale / drag-along** (*cession forcée*): art. L.227-16 lets a SAS's by-laws require a shareholder to sell its shares; failing agreement, the price is set by an expert under art. 1843-4 Code civil. **Temporary inalienability** (*inaliénabilité temporaire*): a lock-up is valid only if **temporary and justified by a serious and legitimate interest** (principle of art. 900-1 Code civil), which is why such clauses are time-bound (e.g. four years). **Tag-along** (*sortie conjointe*) lets a minority follow a selling majority. These are clause families, combined differently by context to form the recurring shapes of pacts.
