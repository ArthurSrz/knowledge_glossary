[Minority interest vs full ownership](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q1415886)

The distinction between holding a minority interest and owning 100% of a company rests on one separation: **the capital invested (money) is not the same thing as the power to decide (control)**. Wikidata formalises this by marking *capital participation* (the money side, a sub-class of ownership) as expressly **different from** *participation in decisions*. A full owner has both — exclusive control, free exit, all the risk and all the reward, and no one able to impose anything or to challenge its choices. A minority holder owns a fraction without the power: no control over decisions, uncertain **liquidity** (no market to resell), a **minority discount** on value, and exposure to being forced to sell (drag-along / [[Forced sale clause]]). French law gives the minority only a thin, threshold-graded floor of protection ([[Blocking minority]], [[Abuse of majority]]), so the substance of its protection is negotiated in a [[Shareholders agreement]]. The full owner, by contrast, falls entirely outside this regime.
