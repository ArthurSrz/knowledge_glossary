The fragment of telegram above contains implicit knowledge that helped trigger a war. It is the Ems Dispatch, written by Wilhelm I in 1870 and then condensed by Bismarck to make clear, through subtext, that Prussia wished to break off diplomatic relations with France. And a diplomatic break means…

That, the knowledge that a diplomatic break implies — without being written anywhere — war, is a wonderful example of implicit, unwritten knowledge. And I think that in 2026 we still run into this when trying to teach machines what is not encoded in data, and rightly so! If the machine doesn't have the data, how could it guess it?

In other words, we run into difficulties precisely because the data ([[data]]) is given, and we ask ourselves less often the question of what is not put into data. The unwritten, the implicit.

To better understand how the implicit works, I propose we dive together into the ontology of the implicit, with wikidata.

## An exploration of the ontology of the implicit

We start with the [[concept]] of implicitness ([[implicitness]]):

> [!definition]
> Implicitness is the property of information that is not expressed, specified, or detailed, but which must be gleaned from context and other assumptions.

![[impliciteness_1.png|300x220]]

We immediately come across the notion of context ([[context]]), but above all the fact that it is a property of information. Here, I immediately suspect a kinship with logical inference ([[inference]]), insofar as the implicit is what is not written, but also everything that cannot be false given everything that has been written.

We then arrive at a first distinction. Within the implicit lies both:
1. everything that has not been said
2. everything that cannot be false given everything that has been written.

Note that 2 is, for all practical purposes, a subset of 1.

## Back to earth

If the implicit is a characteristic of information, then we should be able to make a list of the pieces of information for which this characteristic exists and carries a value. We have to push wikidata here, because it is an ontology oriented toward the abstract, where each [[concept]] tends upward (toward the abstract) and rarely downward (toward the concrete).

After a few forced jumps downward (toward the concrete) in Wikidata, we manage to find examples. These are attached to intermediate concepts, namely:
* [[connotation]]
* [[subtext]]
* [[null morpheme]]
* [[implementation detail]]
* [[de facto]]
* [[implicature]]

## Examples of famous implicits

We finally find some famous examples of the implicit. Among my 3 favorites:

> [!example] [[implicature]]
> The Laconic reply of the Spartans reported by Plutarch. Philip II of Macedon had warned Sparta: "If I enter Laconia, I will raze it to the ground.", and the Spartans replied with a single word: "If."

> [!example] [[Connotation]]
> The word "appeasement" and its interpretation across different historical periods. Before 1938, it was neutral, even positive — a reasonable diplomatic virtue. After Chamberlain in Munich, its [[connotation]] was permanently reversed, becoming synonymous with cowardly capitulation.

> [!example] [[Subtext]]
> The Ems Dispatch (1870). In 1870, Bismarck reworked a diplomatic telegram from Wilhelm I, addressed to France, tightening the tone so that the subtext would read as a deliberate affront to France. The literal content was diplomatic; the implied insult contributed to triggering the Franco-Prussian War.
